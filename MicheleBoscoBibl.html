[ Anonyme ? ] Relación del rigvroso martyrio, qve el Padre Fr. Ioan de Prado de la provincia de San Diego de Sevilla, predicador, y provincial, que fue de la misma provincia, que es de frailes descalços franciscos, padecio en la ciudad de Marruecos ... la qual relacion embió el Governador de Marzagan, al Excelentissimo Señor Duque de Medina Sydonia Sevilla, por Luis Estupiñan 1631   Printed text [ 4 pp. ; 4º ] Juan de Prado, Blessed - Martyrdom - Maroc - XVII century - North Africa - Sacrifice for faith - Christian-Islamic struggle - Franciscan Order - Barbary The account is very short (just 4 pages) and relates the events of Friar Juan de Prado's mission to Morocco in the first months of 1631. The author refers that the Franciscan friar had asked for and obtained the necessary licences to travel to Morocco (Azamor and Marrakech) to give assistance and spiritual relief to Christian captives and to carry out a mission of evangelisation of the Moors, together with his co-friar Matías de San Francisco. But the King of Morocco, Muley Abdel Malik, who had granted safe-conduct to the two friars, had died before their arrival and his brother, who had succeeded him on the throne, did not want to recognise the safe-conduct given by his dead brother and took the friars hostage as his slaves. After keeping them in prison for a month, the king gave them the opportunity to be released, on condition that they left as soon as possible to return to Spain, without preaching the Christian religion nor proselytising. But Juan de Prado did not accept, because he had gone to Africa for that purpose and, instead, began to debate with the king of Morocco and to give his arguments attempting to convince him to convert to Christianity. He told him that man can only be saved in the law of Christ and “condemned the falsehood and deception of the Sect of Mohammed”, through which “all Moors are lost and condemned to Hell”. Then the Moroccan king, enraged, attacked him with a dagger and, in an uncontrollable hatred, struck him several times in the chest. Finally, he ordered the almost lifeless body of the friar to be burnt on a pyre, saying to all the Muslims present: “Whoever wants to go to paradise, let him throw a piece of wood on the fire!”.
The author of the report explicitly speaks of the friar as a “Holy Martyr of Christ”, because he was “condemned to death and burned for the faith” and adds that with his last strength, already engulfed in flames, he continued to preach the Catholic faith until his last breath. Iuan Nogues, 1644 Printed book Martyrdom ; Saints Justo and Pastor This work is a hagiography dedicated to Saints Justus and Pastor, also known as the “Holy Children”, or the “young martyrs”. Saints Justus and Pastor died martyrs in 304 A.D., during the great persecution of Diocletian, as children under the age of ten. They were executed in Alcalá de Henares, by order of the governor Dacian, because they refused to abjure Christianity. In the place where they were martyred, years later a chapel was built to house their remains. After the Muslim invasion, the relics of the holy children were taken by Saint Urbicius to the province of Huesca and to Bordeaux (France); in 1568 part of the remains returned to Alcalá, but most of them were kept in Huesca, to which monument a large part of this work is dedicated. He was probably born in Huesca, Tarragona (Spain) during the latter part of the 3rd century; after being named by the former Bishop Valerius as deacon of the Church of Saragossa, he was commissioned to preach throughout the diocese. He was martyred under the Emperor Diocletian around the year 304. His veneration immediately spread throughout the Church. It reports the story of the Jesuit Baltasar de Torres, who in the early 17th century travelled to Japan, where he began to study the Japanese language. Years later, he was sent by his superiors to the Miaco region to carry out a difficult task of evangelisation: he stayed there for some years, during which he succeeded in converting some Japanese to the Catholic faith, and ministered to the needs and administered the sacraments to the new Christian worshipers. Finally, Father Balthasar suffered martyrdom, being killed on 21 June 1626, at the age of 63. At the time of his martyrdom, he had been in the Society of Jesus for 47 years, and had spent 26 years in Japan, where he acted as an Apostle, as the author says, “to the glory of our Lord, may he be praised in his Saints, for ever and ever”. Printed book   "Topography and General History of Algiers" is a fascinating chronicle written by the Portuguese cleric Antonio de Sosa, who describes, from the perspective of his captivity in the late 16th century, the Algerian society under the splendor of Turkish rule. It is a rare and curious work and contains important information on Christian captivity in late 16th century Algiers. The author wanted his work to be published Anonyme, probably because of the perspective employed by the narrator to describe and judge the life, virtues and sins of the society he observes, but also of the several Christians that converted to Islam and moved Algiers, or the hundreds of Christian captives that decided to convert to Islam having lost the hope of their freedom. The book is of a great complexity but it also presents some inconsistencies (above all in the second part).
